# The "Agentic" Mindset: Silicon Valley's Hottest Buzzword Actually Has a Point

Silicon Valley has a gift for taking a perfectly good concept, wrapping it in jargon, and making everyone cringe. The latest victim: "agentic."

"Are you agentic enough?" asks a recent WIRED headline. LinkedIn is flooded with posts about "agentic individuals" and "agentic leadership." Job descriptions now list "agentic thinking" as a requirement, right between "synergy" and "thought leadership" in the pantheon of corporate meaninglessness.

And yet — hear me out — there's something real underneath the buzzword. Something worth taking seriously.

## What "Agentic" Actually Means

Strip away the tech-bro posturing and "agentic" describes something simple: **the ability to act with autonomy, take initiative, and drive outcomes without waiting for permission or detailed instructions.**

The term comes from psychology — Albert Bandura's concept of "human agency," the capacity to influence your own functioning and life circumstances. It's not new. What's new is *why* it suddenly matters so much.

In the AI era, the nature of work is fundamentally changing. AI handles routine cognitive tasks. It follows instructions. It processes, summarizes, generates, and executes predefined workflows. What it doesn't do — at least not well — is:

- Decide *what* to work on
- Navigate ambiguity without clear parameters
- Take ownership of outcomes when the path is unclear
- Exercise judgment in situations with incomplete information
- Create genuine novelty (not recombination — actual novelty)

These are agentic capabilities. And they're becoming the primary differentiator between people who thrive and people who get automated.

## The Mindset Shift Nobody Talks About

Here's what the LinkedIn gurus miss: being "agentic" isn't a personality trait you either have or don't. It's a **mindset shift** — and for most people, it requires unlearning decades of conditioning.

Think about how most of us were trained:

**School:** Follow the curriculum. Answer the question asked. Stay in your lane. Wait for instructions. Get graded on compliance.

**Corporate work:** Follow the process. Complete assigned tasks. Escalate decisions upward. Stay in your lane. Get reviewed on execution.

**The new reality:** Define the problem. Figure out the approach. Use whatever tools exist. Cross lanes freely. Get evaluated on outcomes.

This isn't just a skills gap. It's a *psychological* gap. Most people have spent 15-20 years being rewarded for following instructions well. Now the game has changed, and following instructions is literally what AI does better than any human.

The shift is from **responsive** to **directive**. From "what should I do?" to "here's what I'm going to do." From waiting for the prompt to writing the prompt.

## Why Most People Struggle With This

Let me be honest about something: the agentic mindset is genuinely hard to develop, and the difficulty is mostly emotional, not intellectual.

### The Fear of Being Wrong

When you take initiative, you own the outcome. If it fails, it's on you. In a follow-instructions world, failure is always partially someone else's fault — the instructions were bad, the process was flawed, the manager didn't give enough context. Agency means giving up that safety net.

I see this constantly in how people interact with AI tools. They want AI to make the decision, then they'll evaluate it. That's not agency — that's delegation of responsibility disguised as efficiency. Real agency means forming your own view first, then using AI to enhance or challenge it.

### The Paralysis of Infinite Options

When someone tells you exactly what to do, the path is clear. When you have access to AI tools that can do almost anything, the question becomes: *what should I even be working on?*

This is the paradox of the AI era. More capability, more paralysis. More options, less clarity. The agentic person isn't the one with the best tools — they're the one who can decide where to point them.

### The Identity Crisis

For many people, their professional identity is built on being good at tasks that AI now handles. The agentic shift requires rebuilding your identity around judgment, initiative, and taste rather than execution. That's not a weekend project. It's an existential reckoning.

## What the Agentic Mindset Actually Looks Like

Let me make this concrete. Here's the difference between standard and agentic approaches to the same situation:

**Situation:** Your company needs to enter a new market.

**Standard approach:** Research the market (or ask AI to). Create a presentation. Present findings to leadership. Wait for a decision. Execute the plan you're given.

**Agentic approach:** Form a hypothesis about *why* this market matters and *how* to enter it. Use AI to stress-test your thinking. Build a prototype or run a small experiment. Come to leadership with results, not just analysis. Drive the decision rather than waiting for it.

**Situation:** You notice a recurring problem in your team's workflow.

**Standard approach:** Report it to your manager. Suggest it be added to the roadmap. Wait for prioritization.

**Agentic approach:** Build a quick solution using AI tools. Test it with the team. Present the working solution alongside the problem. Make it easy to say yes.

The pattern: agentic people **reduce the distance between identifying a problem and solving it.** They don't wait for permission to be useful.

## The Dark Side of "Agentic"

I want to be real about something: the "agentic" rhetoric can be weaponized. And it is being weaponized.

When companies say they want "agentic" employees, sometimes what they mean is: "We want people who do more work without more pay, take on more responsibility without more authority, and don't complain about lack of structure or support."

That's not agency. That's exploitation with a Silicon Valley rebrand.

True agency requires **organizational support**. You can't demand that people take initiative and then punish them when their initiative doesn't align with unspoken expectations. You can't ask for "agentic thinking" but maintain rigid approval chains. The mindset only works in environments that actually tolerate risk-taking.

If your company wants agentic people but has a culture of blame, bureaucracy, and micromanagement — the problem isn't your employees' mindset. It's yours.

## How to Actually Develop Agency

Okay, practical advice. If you want to become more "agentic" (I promise I'll stop with the quotes eventually), here's what actually works:

### 1. Start With Small Bets

You don't develop agency by making one dramatic, bet-the-farm decision. You develop it through hundreds of small, low-stakes choices. Fix something without asking. Ship something small. Suggest a direction instead of asking for one.

### 2. Build Your Judgment Muscle

Agency without judgment is just recklessness. Actively practice forming opinions before consulting AI or colleagues. Write down your prediction, then check it. Track how often you're right. Your judgment improves through deliberate practice, not passive experience.

### 3. Get Comfortable With "Good Enough"

Perfectionists struggle with agency because every self-directed decision feels risky. The agentic mindset accepts that 80% confidence is enough to move. You can course-correct later. Waiting for 100% confidence is its own kind of failure.

### 4. Redefine Your Role as "Outcome Owner"

Stop thinking of yourself as someone who completes tasks. Start thinking of yourself as someone who owns outcomes. The difference is subtle but transformative. Task completers wait for inputs. Outcome owners create them.

### 5. Use AI as an Amplifier, Not a Crutch

The most agentic use of AI: "Here's my plan. What am I missing?" The least agentic: "What should I do?" One multiplies your judgment. The other replaces it.

## The Bigger Picture

Behind all the buzzword cringe, the agentic mindset is really about something ancient: **taking ownership of your life in a world that's changing fast.**

The AI era isn't going to reward people who wait for instructions, no matter how efficiently they execute them. It's going to reward people who decide what matters, take action, and learn from the results.

That's not a tech concept. That's a human one. Bandura knew it decades ago. Stoic philosophers knew it millennia ago. The AI era just makes it impossible to ignore.

So yes, "agentic" is a cringy word. But the mindset it points to? That's the most valuable thing you can develop right now.

Stop waiting for the prompt. Start writing it.
